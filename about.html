About Melissa

I’m a design leader who likes the parts of work most people never see: the systems, patterns, and decisions that make an experience feel clear when they’re done well.

I care about strong design, good judgment, and helping teams build ways of working that last.

I Learned to Notice the Work behind the Work Early

I grew up on a small u-pick farm in central Illinois. Summers meant strawberries, blueberries, early mornings, and a lot happening behind the scenes before customers arrived.

It taught me some early lessons:

  • keep things organized
  • solve problems fast
  • help people find what they need
  • make the experience feel easy, even when it takes work

I didn’t call it design then, but I was already paying attention to how the whole thing held together.

Design Made Sense When I Started Looking Underneath

I’ve always been creative. I studied graphic design in college and became a self-taught photographer along the way.

Visual clarity mattered to me, but I kept wanting to know what made an experience work. UX gave me a way to connect visual thinking with the bigger questions:

  • how people find their way through information
  • where communication breaks down
  • how teams make decisions
  • how good design reduces friction

I Build the Structure around the Work

I still care about the product itself. When the same problem keeps showing up, I also look for a clearer way to handle it next time.

That often means building:

  • design systems
  • shared standards and review paths
  • workflows and documentation
  • training that helps people use the tools well

I like making complex work easier to understand and helping people make better decisions sooner.

A good system should not need a lot of explaining every time it’s used.

Accessibility Changed How I Define Quality

Accessibility became a major part of my work at the University of Illinois. Working alongside people who cared deeply about disability inclusion changed how I saw design.

It stopped feeling like a requirement and became a practical way to:

  • reduce friction
  • create clarity
  • support independence
  • make better experiences for more people

That is still how I think about quality. Accessibility is a major strength in my work, and part of a larger belief that good design should earn people’s trust.

I Want People to Need Me Less over Time

I’m at my best when I’m helping people become more capable, not more dependent.

Some of my favorite moments are when:

  • a designer has a lightbulb moment
  • a team finally connects the dots
  • a process becomes clearer and easier to use
  • someone gains confidence in their own work

I lead through clarity, coaching, and practical support. I care about the work getting better, but I care just as much about the people doing it feeling steady and capable.

If something can be said simply, it probably should be.

Speaking & Teaching

  • Co-presenting on accessibility at a Walmart Enterprise Business Solutions quarterly town hall (2025)
  • Conference sessions at WebCon and IT ProForum on product delivery, design systems, and cross-unit collaboration

What Stays the Same

At the center of everything I do is the same impulse: to make things better, clearer, and more humane.

That is what drew me to design, pulled me toward accessibility, and keeps me interested in leadership and systems today.
