Service blueprint

Historical reconstruction

A reconstruction of the service patterns some teams used to move a touchpoint or change from intake through triage, support, delivery, and portfolio learning.

Service layer
New touchpoint or change
Context and triage
Support plan
Work and review
Checkpoint and learning
Product, design, and engineering team
Share a new surface, linked intervention, or access concern.
Provide the journey, audience, timing, owner, and known constraints.
Confirm the support lane, accountable owner, and delivery window.
Use guidance; participate in design review or manual / assistive-technology QA; resolve agreed findings.
Confirm outcome, new evidence, and the next change trigger.
AX Design leadership
The Group Director, portfolio, or Accessibility Lead creates or routes the touchpoint and its linked intervention.
Assess journey protection, reach, dependencies, and timing with design, product, business, engineering, and the larger accessibility team.
Recommend Baseline or Funded support; the larger accessibility team makes the budget decision.
Provide office hours, design and engineering support, design review, manual / AT QA, or third-party audit support.
Retain the decision, reassess risk, and recommend the next proportionate move.
Accessibility Champions
Surface local changes, questions, and recurring accessibility needs.
Help teams find the right guidance, Slack channel, or support route.
Reinforce accessible ways of working; escalate risks rather than making funding or audit decisions.
Support adoption and route unresolved questions to the Accessibility Lead, design system, or office hours.
Feed recurring gaps and useful practices back into shared guidance.
Line of visibility: the team sees intake, decision, support route, and checkpoint
Backstage decision work
Check for duplicate records and link the intervention to the affected touchpoint.
Validate signals, protected journeys, capacity, and future planning windows.
Sequence work against funded commitments, assessments, and enablement capacity.
Coordinate vendor, platform, design-system, delivery, and third-party support dependencies.
Use portfolio-health review to continue baseline, monitor, audit, or recommend deeper support.
Exception and recovery
Make an urgent or critical release visible before it is lost in delivery work.
Clarify ownership; the Accessibility Lead steers unresolved decisions to the right partners.
When capacity is constrained, stakeholders adjust timing or add capacity.
If critical work ships with known gaps, the Accessibility Lead and Product Lead own the next-sprint remediation and testing plan.
Confirm remediation was completed, tested, and retained as evidence.
Supporting systems and evidence
Touchpoint and linked intervention records
Touchpoint ID, owner, lifecycle, audience, and essential-journey context.
Intervention type: release, feature, content update, migration, customer issue, vendor change, or audit finding.
Support lane, capacity, delivery window, accountable owner, and linked evidence.
Design-review outcomes, manual / AT QA or third-party findings, remediation status, and change notes.
Checkpoint outcome, recurring signals, and reusable guidance for future teams.
Channels, documentation, and enablement
AX Design and Accessibility Slack channels receive questions and route support.
Design-system and specialist Slack channels connect teams to focused expertise.
Confluence holds strategy, Baseline support, guidance, videos, and Figma training resources.
Use automation by default; add design review, manual / AT QA, third-party support, or UX research evidence when proportionate.
Share findings through Slack, office hours, Confluence, components, checklists, and training.

Blueprint boundary: this reconstructs patterns used by some teams; it does not claim every handoff, cadence, or system integration was consistently in place across the organization. The blueprint makes the responsibilities and evidence needed to run the model explicit.
