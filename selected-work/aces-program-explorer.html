College of Agricultural, Consumer and Environmental Sciences (ACES) · 2022-2023

ACES Program Explorer

UX design Interaction design Information architecture UX leadership

I designed and led a scalable program-discovery experience that helped prospective students explore ACES programs by interest instead of by internal department structure.

The visible problem was a long program list. The deeper product challenge was turning department ownership, fast certificate growth, and a catalog that would later reach 126 program options into one coherent discovery experience.

Challenge

Students explored by interest. The college operated by department.

The College of ACES had a growing catalog of majors, minors, certificates, master’s programs, and PhD programs across 9 departments. The old experience presented programs mostly as long degree-type lists, which made exploration difficult on mobile and forced prospective students to understand the college’s internal structure before they could find programs that matched their interests.

As the catalog grew, several challenges emerged:

  • Too many choices created decision paralysis.
  • Users had no way to filter or sort programs.
  • Program discovery relied on browsing long lists organized around institutional categories.
  • Navigation was especially difficult on mobile devices.
  • Interest areas often crossed department boundaries.
  • The college needed a model that could scale as new certificates and program options were added.

A student interested in business, food systems, animals, sustainability, health, or technology might not know which ACES department owned the relevant program, or whether the program existed at all.

Approach

I supplied the product discipline in an environment without a formal product function: framing the problem, shaping the research approach, aligning stakeholders, defining scope, making tradeoffs, and directing implementation.

The project combined user research, information architecture, content strategy, accessibility, and interaction design to create a more flexible experience that could grow as new programs were added.

Working with developers, content strategists, recruiters, advisors, department representatives, and campus stakeholders, we focused on helping prospective students answer a fundamental question:

What programs fit my interests?

The result was a searchable, filterable experience built around student interests rather than the college’s internal map.

What Was Created

Program Discovery Experience

I personally designed the full Program Explorer, including the reusable UI components used in the experience: program cards, filters, sort behavior, detail views, calls to action, the related-programs section, responsive behavior, accessibility expectations, and Drupal content-model requirements.

The final experience let students browse academic offerings, filter by areas of interest, sort results, scan program cards, open in-experience program details, move to related programs, and use direct Apply and Advisor Contact calls to action.

The design supported both desktop and mobile experiences while remaining scalable for future growth.

Information Architecture

A significant portion of the project focused on organizing content in a way that matched user expectations instead of departmental ownership.

Research and testing informed interest-based categories, program naming conventions, department associations, search terminology, and navigation structure. The work also showed where programs needed to belong to more than one mental model.

I facilitated a related-programs mapping workshop with department stakeholders. Each major had a printed sheet, and representatives physically placed program cards to show which options they believed were genuinely related based on student interest and advising patterns.

That exercise turned an abstract IA debate into visible decisions and became the basis for the Explorer’s related-programs feature.

Structured Content System

To reduce maintenance overhead and improve consistency, I defined a structured Drupal content model so each program record could power multiple parts of the experience.

One program record supplied the card, filters, detail view, related-program surfacing, and calls to action. That made the Explorer a scalable content system instead of a hand-maintained collection of one-off pages.

Accessibility-First Design

Accessibility considerations were incorporated from the beginning of the project rather than added later.

Key considerations included a WCAG 2.1 AA target, accessible color contrast, keyboard navigation, focus management, screen reader optimization, responsive typography, meaningful image descriptions, and reduced reliance on animation.

User testing revealed opportunities to further simplify navigation for assistive technology users. One notable improvement was treating each program card as a single interactive element, reducing screen reader verbosity and improving efficiency.

Validation came through internal automated checks, team testing, and manual review with accessibility-focused working group members. This was not a formal third-party accessibility audit.

Research and Testing

Because the primary prospective-student audience was difficult to access directly, we used a practical research approach: card sorting, tree-testing for findability, proxy student participants, interns, small follow-up rounds, advisor and recruiter input, and limited parent feedback.

The research helped validate navigation structures, category labels, and content organization before implementation. One saved findability-testing task for Agricultural and Biological Engineering showed 96% success and 96% directness, an encouraging signal for the direction.

Outcomes

The Program Explorer shipped in roughly 6-8 months and remained live in essentially the same public-facing form years later.

The model supported a catalog that later reached 126 program options across 9 departments. Certificate offerings grew from 16 total certificates to 43 without requiring a redesign of the discovery model.

Site analytics showed users moving from the Explorer into program areas and specific program pages, suggesting the experience was supporting real discovery behavior.

The underlying discovery pattern also later informed a shared program/course repository between Education and Gies College of Business. That is best understood as later influence, not another college adopting the ACES Explorer as-is.

The strongest outcome was durability: the Explorer gave ACES a scalable discovery model that could absorb catalog growth without forcing the college back into hand-maintained program lists.

My Role

I served as acting product lead and UX design lead, while also directly designing the Explorer experience. I owned the product framing, information architecture, interaction model, research direction, accessibility expectations, and structured content requirements.

Two engineers implemented the experience from my designs and product direction, with support from an additional UX designer and a graduate student. I led the stakeholder alignment work that turned department knowledge, research findings, and implementation constraints into a shippable discovery model.

My hands-on work included journey mapping, wireframes, prototyping, card sorting, tree-testing, stakeholder interviews, research synthesis, accessibility review, developer partnership, and launch coordination.

Key Takeaways

This project reinforced that finding information is often a bigger challenge than presenting information. The most important design decisions were not visual. They were organizational.

The hardest part was not designing filters. It was helping 9 departments agree that students do not explore the college the way the college is organized.

By combining research, accessibility, content strategy, and information architecture, we created an experience that helped users move from “I don’t know what I want to study” to “I can see which programs fit my interests.”

It also demonstrated how product strategy and hands-on UX design can create a durable model for complex institutional content.
