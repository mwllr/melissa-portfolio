College of Agricultural, Consumer and Environmental Sciences (ACES) · 2022-2023

ACES Program Explorer

UX design Product strategy Information architecture UX leadership

I turned a sprawling academic catalog into a scalable recruiting and discovery engine, helping prospective students find programs by interest while giving ACES a model that could grow with its offerings.

From Program List To Discovery Engine

ACES had strong academic offerings, but the experience made students decode the college’s internal structure before they could find programs that matched their interests.

The visible problem was a long list of majors, minors, certificates, master’s programs, and PhD programs. The deeper business problem was discoverability: as the catalog grew across 9 departments, valuable programs could stay hidden from the very students most likely to care about them.

I reframed the work from a page redesign into a product strategy problem: how could ACES help students explore by interest, support recruiting conversations, and keep scaling as new programs and certificates were added?

The result was a student-centered discovery experience built around filtering, related programs, structured content, accessible interaction patterns, and clear next steps to apply or contact an advisor.

Challenge

Students were trying to imagine their future; however, the college was showing them its filing system.

ACES had strong academic programs, but the discovery experience did not make that strength easy to see.

  • Programs were organized mostly as long degree-type lists, with limited support for filtering, sorting, mobile exploration, or cross-department discovery.
  • If students could not find relevant programs, ACES risked hiding valuable offerings inside its own structure.
  • A student interested in business, sustainability, animals, food systems, health, technology, or public policy might not know which department owned the relevant program or whether it existed at all.
  • Certificate offerings were expanding quickly, and the old model was already strained. A longer list would only make the experience harder to use and harder to maintain.

ACES needed more than a better page. It needed a scalable way to connect prospective students with the programs most relevant to their interests.

Approach

I reframed the work from “redesign the program list” to “build a discovery model the college could grow with.”

There was no formal product function, so I supplied that discipline while also leading the UX design. I framed the problem, shaped the research approach, defined the scope, facilitated stakeholder decisions, made tradeoffs, and directed the implementation.

The work brought together developers, content strategists, recruiters, advisors, department representatives, college leadership, and campus stakeholders. Each group had legitimate needs. Departments needed accurate representation. Admissions needed clearer recruiting paths. Students needed a way to explore without understanding the college’s internal structure first.

My job was to pull those needs into one coherent model.

We centered the experience around a simple student question:

What programs fit my interests?

That question became the product strategy. It moved the work away from institutional organization and toward student-centered discovery.

What I Designed

I personally designed the full Program Explorer experience, including the program cards, filters, sort behavior, detail views, calls to action, related-programs section, responsive behavior, accessibility expectations, and Drupal content-model requirements.

The final experience allowed students to move from broad interest to a more focused set of options.

  • Browse academic offerings by interest.
  • Narrow a large catalog through filters and sorting.
  • Scan program cards quickly.
  • Open program details inside the experience.
  • Move to related programs.
  • Contact an advisor.
  • Continue toward application paths.

The design turned a static catalog into a guided exploration experience. A filter was just a feature; the outcome was that students could move from uncertainty to a more focused set of options, while the college gained a better way to surface programs that might otherwise stay buried.

Information Architecture

The hardest design problem was not the interface. It was the organization underneath it.

ACES operated through departments, but students explored through interests. Those two models did not naturally match. A student interested in business might belong in Agricultural and Consumer Economics. A student interested in sustainability might have relevant options across several departments. A student interested in food systems, technology, or health might not know where to begin.

I used research, card sorting, tree testing, advisor and recruiter input, stakeholder interviews, and practical facilitation to shape an interest-based IA model. The work showed that programs needed to belong to multiple mental models, not a single rigid category.

Making Related Programs Tangible

The hardest part was not designing filters. It was helping 9 departments align around the idea that students do not explore the college the way the college is organized.

I facilitated a related-programs mapping workshop with department stakeholders. Each major had a printed sheet, and representatives physically placed program cards to show which programs they believed were genuinely related based on student interest and advising patterns.

That exercise turned an abstract IA debate into visible decisions. It became the basis for the Explorer’s related-programs feature and helped cross-department pathways become intentional instead of accidental.

Structured Content System

The Explorer was designed as a system, not a one-off interface.

To make the experience scalable, I defined a structured Drupal content model where one program record could power multiple parts of the Explorer.

A single program record supported the card, filters, detail view, related-program surfacing, advisor calls to action, apply calls to action, and consistent program information across the experience.

That created business value behind the scenes. As the catalog grew, ACES did not have to keep hand-maintaining disconnected lists and pages. The same structured content model could absorb new programs and certificates while preserving a consistent discovery experience.

That mattered because the catalog later reached 126 program options across 9 departments, and certificate offerings grew from 16 to 43 without requiring a redesign of the discovery model.

Accessibility And Quality

Accessibility shaped the interaction model from the beginning.

I designed toward WCAG 2.1 AA expectations, including color contrast, keyboard navigation, focus management, screen reader considerations, responsive typography, meaningful image descriptions, and reduced reliance on animation.

One practical decision was to treat each program card as a single interactive element. That reduced screen reader verbosity and made the experience more efficient for assistive technology users.

In this project, accessibility was not a separate specialty layer. It was part of design quality: making sure the discovery system worked for more students, in more contexts, from the start.

Research And Validation

Because the primary prospective-student audience was difficult to access directly, we used a practical research approach: card sorting, tree testing, proxy student participants, interns, small follow-up rounds, advisor and recruiter input, and limited parent feedback.

The research helped validate the interest-based structure, category language, and findability of the model before implementation.

One saved tree-testing task for Agricultural and Biological Engineering showed 96% success and 96% directness. That gave us a strong signal that the IA direction was working, while still staying honest about the limits of the evidence.

We did not have a controlled baseline-vs-final study, and privacy rules prevented tying Explorer usage directly to downstream admissions outcomes. So I do not claim direct impact.

The stronger, evidence-backed story is that the model supported real discovery behavior, scaled with catalog growth, and stayed durable over time.

Content Accuracy Finding

Along the way, research surfaced a content-accuracy issue outside the design team’s control: two programs listed on the college’s majors pages were officially still minors pending university reclassification. Once confirmed with Admissions, the Explorer reflected the accurate status.

Outcomes

The Program Explorer shipped in roughly 6-8 months and remained live in essentially the same public-facing form years later.

What changed because I was involved:

  • ACES moved from a static program list to a scalable recruiting and discovery engine.
  • Prospective students could explore by interest instead of decoding department structure.
  • The college gained a model that supported a catalog that later reached 126 program options.
  • Certificate growth from 16 to 43 offerings was absorbed without redesigning the discovery model.
  • Parents, students, and the Admissions team consistently described the new experience as easier to navigate and less overwhelming than the old list.
  • Program information became easier to structure, maintain, and reuse.
  • Cross-department discovery paths became intentional instead of accidental.
  • Analytics showed users moving from the Explorer into program areas and specific program pages.
  • The concept later influenced related program/course repository work between Education and Gies College of Business.

The business value was not a single metric. It was a more durable capability: ACES could make more of its academic catalog visible, navigable, and usable as recruiting-facing offerings continued to grow.

Measurement Reflection

The Explorer could not be tied directly to downstream admissions outcomes because of institutional privacy constraints, so I do not claim direct impact.

If I were defining the measurement plan today, I would pair UX signals like baseline task success, mobile completion, filter usage, and related-program click-through with business signals such as reduced advising confusion, faster content maintenance, and avoided redesign work as the catalog grew.

That is the measurement lesson from this project: the most persuasive evidence would connect discovery quality to operational scale, recruiting support, and institutional risk reduction.

Key Takeaway

ACES did not need a prettier program list. It needed a better way to connect students with opportunity.

The Program Explorer helped the college shift from presenting academic offerings to guiding discovery.

For students, that meant moving from “I don’t know where I fit” to “I can see programs that match what I care about.”

For the organization, it meant a growing academic catalog became more visible, more scalable, and more connected to recruiting goals.
