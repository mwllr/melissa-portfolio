College of Agricultural, Consumer and Environmental Sciences (ACES) · 2022-2023

ACES Program Explorer

Designing a scalable program discovery experience that helped prospective students explore academic opportunities with confidence.

Challenge

The College of ACES offered an expanding collection of majors, minors, certificates, and academic programs. Over time, the existing presentation evolved into a long list of options organized primarily by degree type.

As the number of offerings grew, several challenges emerged:

  • Too many choices created decision paralysis.
  • Users had no way to filter or sort programs.
  • Program discovery relied heavily on browsing long lists.
  • Navigation was especially difficult on mobile devices.
  • "Learn More" interactions frequently sent users away from the main experience.

The college needed a more intuitive way for prospective students and families to explore programs and identify areas aligned with their interests.

Approach

Rather than treating the problem as a simple redesign, I approached it as an information discovery challenge.

The project combined user research, information architecture, content strategy, accessibility, and interaction design to create a more flexible experience that could grow as new programs were added.

Working closely with content strategists, developers, recruiters, advisors, and stakeholders, we focused on helping users answer a fundamental question:

What programs fit my interests?

The result was a searchable, filterable experience built around how prospective students think rather than how academic departments are organized.

What Was Created

Program Discovery Experience

A responsive program explorer that allowed users to browse academic offerings, filter by areas of interest, sort results, explore related programs, and access detailed program information.

The design was built to support both desktop and mobile experiences while remaining scalable for future growth.

Information Architecture

A significant portion of the project focused on organizing content in a way that matched user expectations.

Research and testing informed program naming conventions, topic categories, department associations, search terminology, and navigation structure.

Several rounds of testing helped refine category labels and terminology to improve discoverability and reduce confusion.

Content Strategy

To reduce maintenance overhead and improve consistency, the experience was designed around a shared data structure.

This allowed the same program information to be displayed in multiple formats without duplicating content.

Benefits included simplified content management, improved consistency, easier updates, and greater flexibility for future enhancements.

Accessibility-First Design

Accessibility considerations were incorporated from the beginning of the project rather than added later.

Key considerations included WCAG 2.1 AA compliance, accessible color contrast, keyboard navigation, focus management, screen reader optimization, responsive typography, meaningful image descriptions, and reduced reliance on animation.

User testing revealed opportunities to further simplify navigation for assistive technology users. One notable improvement was treating each program card as a single interactive element, reducing screen reader verbosity and improving efficiency.

Research and Testing

User research played a significant role throughout the project.

Methods included Optimal Workshop testing, tree testing, card sorting exercises, stakeholder workshops, recruiter and advisor interviews, and prototype testing.

The team gathered feedback from prospective students, parents, college recruiters, academic advisors, department leaders, and campus stakeholders.

The research helped validate navigation structures, category labels, and content organization before implementation.

Collaboration and Feedback

A key part of the process involved bringing stakeholders into the design process.

Department representatives participated in workshops and card-sorting activities that helped shape the final information architecture.

This collaborative approach not only improved the solution but also increased stakeholder buy-in and support during implementation.

Outcomes

The new Program Explorer transformed a growing collection of academic offerings into a more approachable and discoverable experience.

The project established a scalable framework that could evolve alongside the college's expanding portfolio of programs.

Benefits included:

  • Improved program discoverability
  • More intuitive navigation
  • Better support for mobile users
  • Increased accessibility
  • Stronger alignment between user expectations and content organization
  • A more sustainable content management model

The project also reinforced the value of validating assumptions through research before committing to navigation and categorization decisions.

Key Takeaways

This project reinforced that finding information is often a bigger challenge than presenting information.

The most important design decisions were not visual. They were organizational.

By combining research, accessibility, content strategy, and information architecture, we created an experience that helped users move from "I don't know what I want to study" to "I can see which programs fit my interests."

It also demonstrated how user research can challenge internal assumptions and lead to better navigation structures that reflect how people actually think.
