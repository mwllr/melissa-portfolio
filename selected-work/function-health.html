Accessibility Review Health Data

Making Personal Health Data More Accessible, Actionable, and Trustworthy

A product accessibility opportunity brief exploring how Function Health could strengthen assistive technology support, health data comprehension, and member trust.

Personal health data is only valuable if people can understand it, navigate it, and act on it.

As a Function Health member and accessibility-focused product designer, I reviewed the member experience through the lens of assistive technology, health literacy, and product trust. My goal was not to conduct a full audit, but to identify places where small structural improvements could make complex health information meaningfully easier to use.

  1. Accessible Reach the information without workarounds.
  2. Actionable Understand what changed and why it matters.
  3. Trustworthy Know where to go next.

Health Data Raises the Bar

Function Health gives members access to complex, personal, high-stakes health information. That creates a product responsibility: the experience needs to be easy to move through, easy to interpret, and credible when the stakes feel personal.

Accessibility is not just a compliance concern in this context. It affects whether members can find information, interpret results, compare changes over time, and decide what to ask or do next.

For a health data product, accessibility is product quality.

Review Lens

I looked at the member experience through three questions:

  • Can members navigate the experience independently?
  • Can members understand complex health information clearly?
  • Can the product scale accessibility through reusable patterns instead of one-off fixes?

The biggest opportunity is not only to fix individual accessibility issues. It is to strengthen the structure of the member experience so health data is easier to navigate, interpret, and act on across devices and assistive technologies.

Opportunity Themes

These themes connect individual accessibility concerns to member confidence, fewer dead ends, and patterns the team can reuse.

Screen reader and assistive technology support

Some parts of the experience may not communicate clearly to screen reader users, making it difficult to understand location, available actions, status changes, or how health information is organized.

What I would improve

Improve labels, headings, landmarks, button names, form instructions, and status messages for updates such as result changes, expandable biomarker details, and modal states.

Why it matters

Better support can improve independence, reduce confusion, and make the experience feel more reliable.

Keyboard access and focus management

Core flows should be usable without a mouse or touch input. Inconsistent focus order, visible focus states, modal behavior, or interaction patterns can prevent members from completing tasks.

What I would improve

Audit core flows for keyboard access, logical focus order, visible focus states, modal behavior, skip paths, and mouse-dependent components.

Why it matters

Improved keyboard support reduces task abandonment and makes the experience more resilient across input methods.

Visual readability and contrast

Health data experiences rely on color, hierarchy, cards, status indicators, and charts. If contrast, spacing, type size, or hierarchy are weak, members may struggle to see what matters most.

What I would improve

Strengthen color contrast, readable type sizes, spacing, non-color indicators, and hierarchy across summaries, lab details, recommendations, and dashboard states.

Why it matters

Clearer visual structure can improve comprehension during complex or emotionally sensitive moments.

Data visualization and lab result comprehension

Charts and indicators are powerful, but members also need accessible ways to understand what changed, what is in or out of range, and what they might do next.

What I would improve

Pair charts with accessible summaries, data tables, trend descriptions, plain-language explanations, and next-step guidance for result changes, ranges, and chart filters.

Why it matters

Accessible data patterns help members move from seeing results to understanding meaning and possible action.

Semantic structure and content hierarchy

Headings, groups, labels, regions, and predictable page hierarchy help users scan, navigate, and build a mental model of dense health information.

What I would improve

Create reusable content and component patterns for dashboards, result pages, biomarker details, alerts, recommendations, and educational content.

Why it matters

A stronger semantic foundation can reduce cognitive load, improve consistency, and help the product scale across new journeys.

How I Might Prioritize the Work

Accessibility improvements should begin with the highest-value member flows, then become repeatable patterns for results, trends, alerts, and next steps.

Now

  • Audit core flows
  • Fix blocking assistive technology issues
  • Improve focus states and labels

Next

  • Add accessible chart and result patterns
  • Document design-system guidance
  • Add QA checkpoints

Scale

  • Build accessibility governance into product rituals
  • Measure accessibility maturity
  • Extend patterns across member journeys

What Better Access Could Change

Trust

Members are more likely to trust a product that feels clear, stable, and usable.

Comprehension

Better structure helps members understand complex health information.

Actionability

Accessible summaries, recommendations, and data patterns help members know what to do next.

Scalability

Reusable accessible patterns reduce rework as the product grows and make well-labeled health content easier for future assistive and AI-supported experiences to interpret.

How I Think About This Work

My strength is translating accessibility findings into product priorities, design-system improvements, and cross-functional practices that help teams ship better experiences earlier.

In a product like Function Health, I would approach accessibility as part of product quality: identifying barriers, prioritizing the highest-impact member flows, partnering closely with product and engineering, and creating reusable standards that help accessibility scale with the product.

In a personal health data product, accessibility is not a separate layer. It is part of trust.

Personal health data should be understandable, navigable, and actionable for every member. Accessibility can help Function Health move closer to that goal by making complex information easier to reach, easier to interpret, and easier to trust.
